Sekhemkhet

Sekhemkhet was an ancient Egyptian pharaoh of the Third Dynasty during the Old Kingdom, reigning around 2648–2640 BCE. He was the successor of Djoser, famous for his Step Pyramid, and is thought to have been Djoser's immediate successor. Sekhemkhet's reign was relatively brief, estimated to have lasted only six or seven years, which may explain the incomplete state of his major architectural project, the Buried Pyramid at Saqqara. This pyramid, initially designed to surpass Djoser’s in grandeur, remains unfinished, with its base and substructure partially completed. Despite his short reign, Sekhemkhet left a mark through his ambitious pyramid complex, which includes unique architectural elements such as a descending passage and alabaster sarcophagus. He is attested in inscriptions and seals that depict his Horus name, suggesting he was seen as a powerful ruler. However, details about his reign are scarce due to the lack of extensive records. Archaeological discoveries, such as the remnants of his pyramid and associated artifacts, provide the main insights into his time as king, reflecting continuity in the monumental building traditions of the Old Kingdom.

Kings

Khufu

Khufu, also known as Cheops, was an ancient Egyptian pharaoh who ruled during the Fourth Dynasty of the Old Kingdom, around 2589–2566 BCE. He is most renowned for commissioning the Great Pyramid of Giza, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World and a monumental achievement in engineering and architecture. Despite his association with this iconic structure, much of Khufu's life remains shrouded in mystery, as few historical records about him survive beyond inscriptions and accounts from later periods. Herodotus, a Greek historian writing over two millennia after Khufu's reign, depicted him as a tyrannical ruler, though these accounts are often viewed with skepticism due to their lack of contemporary evidence. Khufu's reputation in Egyptian tradition was more balanced, with some texts portraying him as a capable and ambitious leader who sought to immortalize his legacy. His pyramid, originally standing at 146.6 meters (481 feet), reflects the centralized power and organizational skills of the Old Kingdom, showcasing the resources and labor coordination under his rule. Although his mortuary complex included other structures like subsidiary pyramids and temples, his burial site has never been conclusively identified. Khufu's family played a significant role in his dynasty's stability, with several of his sons, including Djedefre and Khafre, succeeding him as pharaohs. Artifacts attributed to Khufu, such as a small ivory statue found at Abydos, provide a rare glimpse into his reign but highlight the scarcity of surviving records. His legacy endures through the awe-inspiring pyramid and the enduring fascination it inspires.

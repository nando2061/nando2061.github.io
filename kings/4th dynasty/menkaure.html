Kings

Menkaure

Menkaure was an ancient Egyptian pharaoh of the Fourth Dynasty, best known for his role in constructing the smallest of the three main pyramids at Giza. Reigning around 2530 BCE, he succeeded his father, Khafre, and was known for a reign characterized by a sense of justice and benevolence, as suggested by later traditions. Menkaure’s pyramid, though smaller than those of Khufu and Khafre, was distinguished by its intricate design and use of granite for its casing stones, reflecting both technical innovation and aesthetic refinement. His mortuary complex included a rich array of statues, many of which portray Menkaure alongside gods or his queen, symbolizing his divine authority and partnership with the gods. These statues exhibit a remarkable level of craftsmanship and provide valuable insight into Old Kingdom artistic and cultural ideals. Menkaure was celebrated for his piety and is remembered in later Egyptian texts as a ruler who sought to act in harmony with Ma'at, the principle of truth and balance. Although his rule is less well-documented than some of his predecessors, his legacy endures as a key figure in ancient Egypt’s Golden Age.

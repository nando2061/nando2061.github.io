Kings

Djedefre

Djedefre, also known as Radjedef, was an ancient Egyptian pharaoh of the Fourth Dynasty during the Old Kingdom, succeeding his father, Khufu, the builder of the Great Pyramid at Giza. He is notable for being the first Egyptian king to incorporate the name of the sun god Ra into his royal titulary, marking an early emphasis on the solar cult that would grow more significant in later periods. Djedefre’s reign, is shrouded in mystery due to limited surviving evidence and his apparent break from tradition. Instead of continuing pyramid construction at Giza, he built his pyramid at Abu Rawash, though it now lies in ruins, leading some to speculate whether it was unfinished or intentionally dismantled. His legacy is ambiguous, with scholars debating whether his choice of a new site reflected a theological shift or practical concerns. His reign may also have seen internal disputes, as there is evidence of conflicts within the royal family, particularly with his successor and brother, Khafre. Despite this, Djedefre's contribution to Egyptian history includes the promotion of solar worship and advancements in art and royal iconography, as seen in exquisite statues attributed to his reign. His reputation in antiquity and beyond has been mixed, partly due to his relative obscurity compared to other pharaohs of his dynasty.

Hor-Aha

Hor-Aha, considered the second pharaoh of Egypt's First Dynasty (circa 31st century BCE), is often credited as a key figure in uniting Upper and Lower Egypt after the reign of Narmer, who is traditionally associated with this unification. His name means "Horus the Fighter," reflecting his association with the falcon god Horus. Hor-Aha continued the development of centralized administration, religious institutions, and urban planning. He is also linked to significant early advancements in Egyptian culture, including the establishment of religious practices. Some records associate his reign with the founding of Memphis, a key city in ancient Egypt. Hor-Aha’s reign marked the consolidation of power and the strengthening of the monarchy as a divine institution. His burial site is likely in Abydos, the royal necropolis for early Egyptian rulers.

Ivory label

Inscription bearing Hor-Aha's serekh together with a Nebty-name expressed with the game-board hieroglyph, which could be read mn. sometimes thought to refer to Menes/Narmer

Ebony label

Fragmented ebony label of Hor-Aha relating a visit of the king to the shrine of the goddess Neith of Sais in the Delta, British Museum.

Seal of Hor-Aha

Clay seal fragment bearing Hor-Aha's serekh together with ḥ and t signs, perhaps meant to signify a personal name Htj

Ivory label

Ivory label bearing the serekh of Hor-Aha. It reports the victory over the "arch-using Setjet-folks" (center) and the visit at the domain "Horus thrives with the cattles" (right).
